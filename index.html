ABOUT ME!

I am a robotics and control systems engineer working at the intersection of autonomy, multi-robot coordination, reinforcement learning, and machine perception. My work explores how classical control theory and modern machine learning combine to build interpretable and reliable autonomous systems. In addition, I also design lightweight experimental platforms and simulation environments to study navigation, coordination, and decision-making in robotic systems. Beyond formal showcase work, I enjoy experimenting with autonomous agents in unconventional environments, from ultra-lightweight drone simulators to reinforcement-learning bots in classic open-source game engines such as Doom and Quake III Arena. These explorations often serve as experimental sandboxes for testing ideas in autonomy, control, and learning.

If you are working on interesting ideas in robotics, autonomy, or intelligent systems, or enjoy experimenting with unconventional autonomy work, feel free to reach out (see contact links at the bottom of the page).

PPO-driven Swarm Control

PPO-driven Swarm Control studies whether a learned local navigation policy can be lifted into a coherent multi-agent system when combined with classical coordination mechanisms. The central issue is that a PPO policy trained on local observations can learn useful directional behavior, but naive replication across agents does not produce a valid swarm controller, leading to clustering, redundant coverage, and unsafe proximity. Rather than treating reinforcement learning as a complete solution, the framework introduces a hybrid controller in which PPO acts as a local navigation primitive while classical multi-robot components impose structure. The system combines learned actions with artificial potential fields for spacing and field shaping, and a graph-based consensus layer for directional coherence, forming a controlled composition rather than an end-to-end policy. The evaluation is deliberately structured and analysis-driven. Starting from a single-agent PPO baseline, the work incrementally introduces repulsion and consensus layers, comparing their effects on coverage, safety, and spatial organization under identical conditions. The focus is on how each component reshapes behavior, not on claiming universal performance gains. This work is therefore positioned as a hybrid swarm-control analysis study, not a fully trained multi-agent RL system. It does not claim global convergence or optimality. Instead, it establishes that combining learned local policies with classical coordination produces measurable improvements in safety and coherence, and identifies conditions under which each layer contributes. The result is a modular, interpretable framework for studying multi-robot coverage through controlled hybridization.

Reflectivity-Augmented LiDAR Scene Understanding for Robotic Perception

Reflect-Aug-Seg studies whether a simple reflectivity-style transformation of raw LiDAR intensity can expose more structured and stable behavior for semantic scene understanding. The core issue is that raw intensity is not a clean surface descriptor, but is entangled with range, geometry, and sensing conditions, limiting its direct semantic use in practice. Instead of attempting full reflectivity recovery, the framework introduces a lightweight pseudo-reflectivity signal from intensity and range, treated as a controlled transformation. The goal is not physical recovery, but to test whether this signal behaves more consistently than intensity across semantic structure and short-horizon motion windows. The evaluation is deliberately scoped. Using SemanticKITTI, the system analyzes class-wise distributions, separability, and temporal stability across small motion windows, supported by visualization and statistical inspection. The focus is on signal behavior, not on training a full model or claiming end-to-end performance gains. This work is positioned as a perception analysis study, not a complete system. It does not claim calibrated reflectivity or universal improvement. Instead, it establishes whether reflectivity-inspired augmentation provides a measurable but limited structural advantage over raw intensity, and where that advantage holds or breaks down across scenarios. The result is a modular, interpretable pipeline for studying reflectivity-aware cues in LiDAR perception.

MicroUAV-2D

MicroUAV-2D is an ultra-lightweight UAV simulation sandbox engineered for rapid experimentation with vision-driven navigation and autonomy algorithms. The platform models a downward-facing aerial vehicle operating over overhead imagery, exposing a clean perception-to-action interface where navigation policies interact directly with the drone’s sensor footprint through configurable field-of-view observations. Built entirely on a minimal NumPy and OpenCV stack, the simulator delivers deterministic observation extraction, stable border handling, and real-time visualization without relying on heavyweight robotics frameworks, physics engines, or GPU infrastructure. The entire simulation core is exceptionally compact, consisting of roughly 17 KB of source code (excluding the base map image), making it dramatically smaller than conventional robotics simulators that typically span hundreds of megabytes. This extreme simplicity enables instant startup, rapid iteration, and frictionless deployment across machines while preserving the essential perception–action loop required for aerial autonomy research. By stripping simulation down to the fundamental interaction between observation, decision, and motion, MicroUAV-2D serves as a fast prototyping environment for reinforcement learning policies, navigation strategies, and perception-guided control systems for lightweight UAV platforms. Its minimal architecture makes it ideal for researchers and engineers who need a fast, transparent sandbox to prototype aerial autonomy without the overhead of complex simulation ecosystems.
